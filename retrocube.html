RetroCube

From broken GameCube to complete retro-game machine

Motivation

Do I really need to outline the motivation for this project? Retro games are timeless classics and often possess a level of charm and nostalgia not found in modern creations. Playing these games on a PC can be difficult though, as you'll typically need a different emulation program installed for each console you wish to emulate, not to mention the fact that playing on a keyboard is never the same as having a good-ol'-fashioner controller in your hands.

A Raspberry Pi is the perfect device to consolidate your digital collection of retro games*. In fact, there is a version of it's OS readily available for just this purpose - RetroPie. It features support for a wide variety of retro systems, as well as various usb game controllers

I've always been a fan of the Nintendo GameCube, and the system seemed like it would offer adequate internal space once the original guts were cleared out. With this in mind, I dove in with the goal of having a fully functional GameCube housing filled with retro gaming internals.

* Note: It is your responsibility to acquire & use digital copies of your retro games in a legal manner. I take no responsibility for consequences stemming from your searching for or use of pirated games or other software.

Parts required

  • Nintendo GameCube (preferably a broken one - if it's still working, let it live!)
  • Raspberry Pi - A model 3 will get you the best performance and make the software setup much easier
  • Wii U GameCube USB adapter - I used an official Nintendo adapter, but third-party ones such as the MayFlash will also work
  • MicroUSB power supply - I recommend one that supplies at least 1.5A, to make sure it can support the peripherals
  • Small tool set, particularly a small hex socket and phillips heads
  • Soldering kit - Iron, solder, flux, wire, etc.
